How a Formula 1 IT director balances innovation and stability at 200 mph

Mercedes-AMG Petronas processes more data in a single race than most enterprises see in a month. Michael Taylor explains how his 18-person team shifts gears between experimentation, execution, and governance — and knows when to let humans take the wheel.

Michael Taylor has spent 25 seasons with the Mercedes-AMG Petronas F1 team, working his way through nearly every IT role — trackside support, engineering systems, business transformation. Today, as IT director, he leads an 18-person team running one of the most data-intensive operations anywhere.

The scale is startling: each car rolls out of the garage carrying 300 sensors and, once running, generates more than a million data points every second. Engineers mine that telemetry for the fractions of a second that separate winning from losing. "The key metric in our sport is the stopwatch," Taylor says — F1 has been data-centric since the 1950s, but now the team instruments everything, on the principle that if you can measure it, you can improve it.

The hard part isn't collecting data — F1 has streamed live telemetry since the 1980s. It's making decisions at speed while keeping the governance that holds a complex, high-stakes operation together. For CIOs under pressure to move fast on AI while managing risk, security, and data quality, Taylor's pit-lane lessons offer a framework.

Innovation vs. stability: 'a constant battle'

In most enterprises the tension between innovation and control plays out over quarters or years. In F1 it happens weekly, and Taylor admits the team doesn't always get it right — which is exactly why he leans on people with deep industry experience when weighing changes.

The operation runs in two distinct modes. Between races, at the Brackley, UK factory — a 60,000-square-meter technology campus where the championship-winning cars are designed, built, and improved — there's room for project work and experimentation. As a race weekend approaches, everything shifts to pure execution: no changes that could affect the engineers. The same agility that drives innovation during the week must yield to stability when results are on the line.

"If security gets in the way of the business, the business will find ways to work around it. The job is to make the secure path the easiest path."

Michael Taylor · IT Director, Mercedes-AMG Petronas

Modernizing at racing speed

The team had run SAP since 1999 — it underpins the entire design-to-track process, from design release through planning, procurement, manufacturing, and testing to reassembling the car trackside. When it came time to modernize, they treated the migration like a pit stop: planned to the second. They signed on to RISE with SAP in December 2024 and targeted go-live during the sport's mandatory two-week August shutdown, "the perfect window to make changes."

They finished eight weeks early. Taylor credits a culture of control shaped by the sport's time-bound nature: rather than hand things to large systems integrators who "dip their toes in and disappear," his 18-person team taps SAP's expertise for specific problems, then takes back ownership.

The secure path must be the easiest path

IP in F1 has a short shelf life — once a new component is photographed in the pit lane, rivals can see it. The real advantage, Taylor argues, is everything behind the part: the thinking, modeling, simulation, failure modes, and development direction. Protecting that demands an offensive posture — his security chief reports directly to him, and the team actively probes its own defenses, thinking and working like a hacker.

In an engineering culture where people are empowered to move fast, heavy-handed security backfires. The lesson: don't slow the organization down; make the secure path the path of least resistance.

Humans in the loop

The team is piloting AI across the organization — machine learning for simulation, agentic workflows in production planning, coding copilots — but Taylor is resisting the urge to rush, expecting to decide what to scale within six to ten months. He remains firmly committed to human oversight: the real benefit comes when AI complements the workforce, not replaces its judgment.

AI has also raised the bar for data governance. "Good enough" now means stronger visibility, cleaner permissions, and clearer ownership — being deliberate about what data AI is allowed to touch.

Start with consequence

Taylor's advice for CIOs elsewhere is deceptively simple: "Start with consequence, not technology." In financial services the consequence might be customer harm or a regulatory breach; in healthcare, patient safety; in F1, lost competitive advantage. Once the consequence is clear, you can decide what needs the strongest control, what needs monitoring, what needs retention — and what just needs better hygiene.

It's a lesson from 25 seasons at the edge of the technically possible, where decisions happen in milliseconds and the margin between success and failure is measured in fractions of a second.

Key takeaways
  • Run two modes deliberately — experiment between races, freeze changes near race weekend.
  • Own your core systems; use vendors for targeted expertise, then take back the reins.
  • Make the secure path the easiest path, or the business will route around it.
  • Keep humans in the loop — scale AI only after structured pilots prove value.
  • Start with consequence, not technology, when setting governance strength.
